In the early twentieth century, ciphering systems were widely in use as they enabled the Western Ally countries during World War II to read and understand substantial amounts of Morse-coded radio communications of the Axis powers. However, even after multiple efforts, these systems failed to break the codes that were enciphered using the Nazi encryption machine Enigma. After years of trial and error, Polish mathematician Marian Rejewski broke the code of Enigma using only limited documentation obtained by French military intelligence.

Less than a decade after breaking the Enigma, British computer scientist Alan Turing challenged the world with one of the simplest yet perplexing questions of all time - Can machines think? In 1950, he published his paper Computing Machinery and Intelligence and proposed the Turing Test which he fondly called an imitation game which tests whether a computer could fool humans into thinking that they were communicating with another human being. This established a strong base for the vision of artificial intelligence(AI) - an advancement that allows systems to mimic or even outperform human cognitive abilities.

Since then, the field of AI has been seeing a constant boom and this growth has been enabled by exponentially faster computers and large datasets. Today, AI forms the basis of the world's popular applications like voice recognition, self-driven automobiles, controlling robots, and android assistants.

From the above-mentioned observations, one thing is pretty clear that the growth rate of AI in the US is immensely higher than that of Europe. The primary reason behind this is that the United States has access to large data sets for training machine-learning algorithms. Europe, on the other hand, has strict data protection rules that safeguard people's privacy. These strict data protection laws limit the number of resources available to train AI systems leading to uncertainty on whether Europe will ever be able to compete with the US in terms of the growth of AI by generating very complex algorithms. The application of AI systems' knowledge to actual problems, rather than academic concerns, drives the improvement of AI systems. Countries will most likely achieve progress by deploying AI in domains where they currently excel.
